1 The Right to Start a Business

Official Transcript of the Committee on the Socio-Political Fabric, Rayburn House Office Building, Room 2123, Faithfully Recorded and Annotated for Posterity1

1.1 PRELIMINARY NOTES FOR THE RECORD

The Rayburn House Office Building had been constructed in 1965 by a government that believed, with the particular optimism of the postwar era, that the future would require a great many large rooms for important meetings. It had accordingly been given large rooms. The meetings were still happening. The question of whether they were important was one the building had learned, over sixty years, not to ask.2

The fluorescent lights in Room 2123 hummed at a frequency that could not technically be called despair but was certainly in the neighborhood, renting a studio apartment there and keeping odd hours.

1.2 MEMBERS PRESENT

1.2.1 ELECTED REPRESENTATIVES

CHAIRMAN NEW WASHING MACHINE (R-IL): A Congressman of the libertarian-adjacent persuasion who had read the Constitution once and felt personally wronged by approximately sixty percent of it. He shuffled papers the way other men did push-ups: compulsively, and with the suggestion of unresolved aggression.

SENATOR SHMURPLE-BERRY YOGURT (D-DE): A five-term Senator that inspired the hit reality television show, “Survivor”. He had outlasted four presidents, three recessions, and one very serious ethics inquiry by simply never doing anything that couldn’t be described as “measured” by pundits his donors watched regularly. His portfolio was up 22% this quarter, mostly in surveillance infrastructure and artisanal bunker manufacturing.3 His tie was the color of compromise. He had worn it to four funerals and twelve fundraising dinners and had stopped feeling the difference around the third funeral.

1.2.2 WITNESSES

ZUCCHINI EL GOURDO, of Brooklyn, New York: Unaffiliated voter, nonprofit management studies graduate dressed in the specific way of a person who has thought carefully about appearing to not have thought carefully about it. He had a prepared statement. He had bullet points. He had, in the second paragraph, a genuinely insightful observation about closed primary systems that no one in this room was going to quote. He had also, though he would not have admitted this, a faint and stubborn hope. He carried it the way you carry an umbrella in a city where it almost never rains: awkwardly, somewhat embarrassingly, and unwilling to leave it behind.

PRINCESS MONSTER TRUCK, of Baton Rouge, Louisiana: millennial, twelve-year hospitality veteran, driver of a 2009 Chevy Silverado with a cracked windshield that State Farm had classified as “cosmetic damage” three consecutive times.4 She had a manila folder containing three insurance claim denials, two appeals, one letter from a state regulator that said essentially nothing in four paragraphs, and a printout of Louisiana House Bill 423 annotated in red pen with words that were, technically, opinions. She had placed the folder on the table in front of her- both hands rested upon on it like a executioner resting their hands on the pommel of an axe. She had the expression of a person who had driven eleven hours and was, therefore, done with preamble.

1.3 OPENING STATEMENTS

CHAIRMAN NEW WASHING MACHINE: The Committee will come to order.

“Order”, in terms of the “ordering” principle of this institution appears to be the organized redistribution of wealth from people with the least- to the people who have the most, while we all describe it as something else. I recognize the Senator from Delaware.

SENATOR SHMURPLE-BERRY YOGURT: Thank you, Mr. Chairman. It is a pleasure to be here, or more precisely, it is a necessity to be seen here, a distinction this institution has long since stopped making. The American people—

PRINCESS MONSTER TRUCK: I drove eleven hours.

SENATOR SHMURPLE-BERRY YOGURT: —are the backbone of this great nation—

PRINCESS MONSTER TRUCK: My windshield has a crack in it the size of my forearm and State Farm called it cosmetic damage.

SENATOR SHMURPLE-BERRY YOGURT: —and your voices are essential to the democratic—

PRINCESS MONSTER TRUCK: I have the letters.

[Witness places a manila folder on the table. The Senator leaned slightly toward his aide.]

SENATOR SHMURPLE-BERRY YOGURT: (to aide, in a register he believed was quieter than it was) Can we get her a sedative? Or a small cabinet position in the Department of Agriculture?

[The aide typed something. Nothing happened. This is the nature of the Department of Agriculture.]

CHAIRMAN NEW WASHING MACHINE: Let’s swear in our witnesses and proceed. We have limited time before the next recess, a subsequent recess, and a vote on something that will be described as historic and will not be.

1.4 TESTIMONY OF MR. ZUCCHINI EL GOURDO

CHAIRMAN NEW WASHING MACHINE: Mr. El Gourdo, you may proceed.

MR. ZUCCHINI EL GOURDO: Thank you. I’ll be brief because I have to get back to Brooklyn to confirm whether my landlord raised my rent during this opening statement, which is not a joke, or not entirely a joke, which is itself a kind of joke.

I represent something like 1.1 million unaffiliated voters in New York City — the fastest-growing registration category in the state, locked out of a closed primary system that decides every meaningful race before the rest of us see a ballot. We are highly educated, deeply embedded in the economic engine of the city, and entirely alienated from the binary machinery of modern American politics. Sixty-five percent of voters believe Congress is out of touch with ordinary people. I want to say, for the record, that I think that number is optimistic.

Specifically today, I want to talk about what it actually costs to build something in this country. I tried to start a nonprofit — urban gardening, community food access, nothing that should frighten anyone. The average time to obtain the relevant permits in New York City is fourteen months.

SENATOR SHMURPLE-BERRY YOGURT: Wonderful. And you’re from — Queens?

MR. ZUCCHINI EL GOURDO: Brooklyn.

SENATOR SHMURPLE-BERRY YOGURT: (nodding in a way that suggested Brooklyn confirmed something he had long suspected about Brooklyn) Mm. I had a very good piece of salmon there in 2019. Wonderful borough.

MR. ZUCCHINI EL GOURDO: Fourteen months, Senator. The average small business in America fails within its first year. We are regulating people out of their own ambitions before they have sold a single thing. The system does not feel like a right. It feels like a subscription service I cannot afford, administered by people who have never tried to use it.5

CHAIRMAN NEW WASHING MACHINE: You mentioned fourteen months. You know how long it takes to register a business in Estonia?

MR. ZUCCHINI EL GOURDO: …No?

CHAIRMAN NEW WASHING MACHINE: Eighteen minutes. Online. (produces a printed Wikipedia page with the practiced efficiency of a man who keeps Wikipedia printouts the way other men keep business cards) Estonia looked at the administrative state and concluded: what if we simply didn’t. And now they have a functional digital society and we have fourteen months and a drainage assessment fee.

PRINCESS MONSTER TRUCK: I don’t know why Estonia is always the example.

CHAIRMAN NEW WASHING MACHINE: Because Estonia got it right and it haunts me.

PRINCESS MONSTER TRUCK: Fair.

SENATOR SHMURPLE-BERRY YOGURT: I think we can all agree that the regulatory environment presents certain structural challenges that a bipartisan framework could—

MR. ZUCCHINI EL GOURDO: My father’s venture capital firm did an internal analysis last year. The best return on investment in their entire portfolio was regulatory influence. Buying access to permitting agencies. Better than any direct investment in an actual small business. He showed me the numbers. He thought it was funny.

[A brief silence, during which the Senator found several things to look at that were not Mr. El Gourdo.]

1.5 TESTIMONY OF PRINCESS MONSTER TRUCK

CHAIRMAN NEW WASHING MACHINE: Ms. Monster Truck. The floor is yours.

PRINCESS MONSTER TRUCK: Are we done with the polite part? Good.

[She opened the folder.]

I’ve been in hospitality for twelve years. I know food. I know how to run a line at full capacity on a Saturday night when the dishwasher quits and the health inspector walks in at seven pm. My sister and I have been planning a food truck for three years. Po-boys and smoked boudin. Something real. Something ours.

We saved eleven thousand dollars. We found a truck. We started pulling permits.

Louisiana requires a state health certificate, a city business license, a fire inspection certification, and proof of a licensed commissary kitchen — which can’t be substituted with your own kitchen regardless of its equipment, because the certification costs four thousand dollars and takes four to seven months. Then a Department of Revenue seller’s permit, a sales tax registration, and in New Orleans, a separate French Quarter vendor permit for any street within two blocks that costs twelve hundred dollars and has a waiting list that has not moved since 2022.

[She turned a page.]

Meanwhile, Governor Landry signs the “No Pay, No Play” insurance reform. The $100,000 auto threshold. The Housley Presumption, gone. You tell us it’s to prevent frivolous lawsuits. My premiums went up fifteen percent the following quarter. I am currently deciding between fixing my alternator and buying insulin, which is not a choice that should exist in a country with this much money in it, and yet here we are, and there it is.

You want to talk about why turnout in Louisiana was twenty-three percent? That is why. Because the people who were supposed to represent us are busy being the HR department for a company that’s going bankrupt, and some of us have started to notice.

[She closed the folder.]

That is what I wanted to say about the right to start a business.

1.6 THE SYNTHESIS6

[A silence settled over the room of the kind that forms when something true has been said in a place that is not accustomed to it. The fluorescent lights hummed. They had hummed through hearings on Vietnam, Watergate, the deficit, healthcare, the financial collapse, and a great many working groups. They intended to keep humming. They had no opinions. This was, in its way, a comfort, and also not a comfort at all.]

SENATOR SHMURPLE-BERRY YOGURT: The commissary kitchen requirement is — yes, that’s something we’ve been examining—

PRINCESS MONSTER TRUCK: With what outcome?

SENATOR SHMURPLE-BERRY YOGURT: —as part of a broader working group—

PRINCESS MONSTER TRUCK: What working group?

SENATOR SHMURPLE-BERRY YOGURT: —that is currently in the process of convening—

PRINCESS MONSTER TRUCK: I drove eleven hours.

SENATOR SHMURPLE-BERRY YOGURT: (quietly) Yes. You’ve mentioned.

PRINCESS MONSTER TRUCK: I’ll keep mentioning it.

[The Senator looked at the folder. He had been in politics for a long time. He had developed, over that time, a very precise internal instrument for detecting when a moment required the appearance of being moved by it. He had also a second, smaller, considerably less-exercised instrument that occasionally registered when he was actually moved by something. The second instrument had not had much practice lately. The commissary kitchen requirement was doing something to the second instrument. He was not entirely sure how he felt about this.]

SENATOR SHMURPLE-BERRY YOGURT: (to aide, very quietly) Find out which subcommittee handles commissary kitchen certification requirements.

[The aide looked this up. It was four subcommittees. The Senator chose not to mention this.]

CHAIRMAN NEW WASHING MACHINE: Here is what I keep arriving at. You both came here. You know how this system actually works — or doesn’t — better than most of the people in this building who are paid to know it. You’re not asking for charity. You’re asking the machinery of the state to get out of the way of the thing you’re willing to bleed for. That should be — it should be — the most obvious, least partisan, most achievable thing we could agree on.

[He looked at the Senator.]

And instead it’s going to go to a working group, and then a subcommittee, and then a recess, and then an election cycle, and somewhere in Louisiana a man who owns a yacht called Tax Shelter is going to file his quarterly returns in a condition of profound personal comfort, and he will not think about the food truck once.

SENATOR SHMURPLE-BERRY YOGURT: That is an uncharitable characterization of the legislative process.

CHAIRMAN NEW WASHING MACHINE: Is it wrong?

[The lights hummed.]

[Somewhere down the hall, a lobbyist was having an excellent lunch and not thinking about commissary kitchens.]

[The Senator did not answer.]

[This was, in its way, an answer.]

MR. ZUCCHINI EL GOURDO: (quietly, to Princess Monster Truck) We don’t want a reboot, you know. We just want a door that isn’t locked from the inside.

PRINCESS MONSTER TRUCK: (equally quietly) And fix my damn insurance before I lose my mind.

MR. ZUCCHINI EL GOURDO: Also that.

PRINCESS MONSTER TRUCK: Is the Estonia thing real?

MR. ZUCCHINI EL GOURDO: It’s Wikipedia. So. Possibly.

PRINCESS MONSTER TRUCK: I’ll take possibly.

1.7 ADJOURNMENT

CHAIRMAN NEW WASHING MACHINE: The Committee will take these testimonies under advisement. We will produce a white paper. It will be twelve pages. Seven people will read past page four. The rest of the country will continue to stare at the flickering lights of their dwindling bank accounts and draw their own conclusions, several of which will be correct. Meeting adjourned.7

1.8 ADDENDUM: SUBSEQUENT EVENTS, FILED FOR THE RECORD

On the train back to Brooklyn — delayed again, indefinitely, for reasons the MTA described as “signal issues” and everyone understood to mean something much older and less fixable — Zucchini El Gourdo wrote a letter. Not a policy brief. Not a Substack post. A letter, to someone who knew a city councilperson who had been, apparently, asking why the unaffiliated numbers kept climbing and nobody showed up. He was not, he thought, hopeful exactly. He was something in the neighborhood of hopeful. Close enough to feel the weather.

On the drive back to Louisiana, Princess Monster Truck called her sister.

“We’re still doing it,” she said.

“After all that?”

“Especially after all that. But I need you to look into what they actually enforce on commissary certification versus what’s technically required. Because I have a feeling those are two very different numbers.”

Her sister laughed. It was the laugh of someone who had known this voice for thirty years and recognized what it sounded like when it got like this: past angry, into something harder and more durable than angry, something that had driven eleven hours and brought the folder and was going to keep mentioning it until something moved.

The crack in the windshield caught the last of the sunset at an angle that turned it briefly into something that looked almost like a map.

Maps are just descriptions of where you are.

What matters is where you’re going.

The food truck opened in the spring. It was called THE ELEVEN HOUR. The boudin was excellent. A man from a regulatory compliance newsletter ate there on the third week and wrote a piece about how commissary kitchen certification requirements in Louisiana were being enforced inconsistently across parishes in ways that suggested the regulation itself was due for review. Two thousand people read it. One was on a working group.

This is not how change happens.

Except that sometimes, rarely, against considerable odds and the general institutional preference for things remaining as they are, it is.

Somewhere in Delaware, Senator Shmurple-Berry Yogurt’s second instrument — the small one, the underexercised one — registered something it couldn’t quite name. He didn’t do anything about it. Not immediately. But the following Tuesday he ate a po-boy from a food truck parked outside the Capitol in contravention of approximately four separate permitting regulations. It was very good. He did not mention the permits.

This was not progress.

It was also not nothing.

The lights hummed on.

They always do.
