The Official Transcript of the Committee on the Socio-Political Fabric

Faithfully Recorded and Annotated for Posterity1

PRELIMINARY NOTES FOR THE RECORD

There is a particular kind of document that humans produce in response to chaos.

Not a solution — solutions require agreement on the nature of the problem, which has been tabled for further review. No: the document produced in response to chaos is a record, a document says: this happened. I was here. I wrote it down.

The document does not say, because it doesn’t need to: *and therefore it is not entirely out of my hands.2

MEMBERS PRESENT

CHAIRMAN NEW WASHING MACHINE (R-IL): A Congressman of the libertarian-adjacent persuasion whose fury at the machinery of the state had, over twelve years in office, become so central to his public identity that it was no longer entirely possible — not for his constituents, not for his staff, and on certain late evenings not entirely for him — to locate where the fury ended and the performance of fury began. He shuffled papers the way other men did push-ups: compulsively, and with the suggestion of unresolved aggression.3

SENATOR SHMURPLE-BERRY YOGURT (D-DE): A five-term Senator of the variety political scientists describe as a “survivor,” which in this context means something subtly different than it does everywhere else.4 His portfolio was up 22% this quarter, weighted toward domestic surveillance infrastructure and artisanal bunker manufacturers. His tie was the color of bipartisan compromise. He had worn it to four funerals and six fundraising dinners and had somewhere around the third funeral stopped being able to tell the difference.

He had, buried beneath the metrics, a small and underexercised instrument — not quite conscience, not quite regret, something more like a tuning fork struck a long time ago, still faintly vibrating — that occasionally registered something when the metrics weren’t watching. He had learned not to think about it. He was a very accomplished not-thinker-abouter of things. This was, more than any single vote or alliance, the actual engine of his survival.5

WITNESSES

MR. ZUCCHINI EL GOURDO, of Brooklyn, New York: Unaffiliated voter. Nonprofit management studies graduate. Son of a venture capitalist, which he had declined to become through an act of will that had felt, at the time, like freedom, and which he was still in the process of determining whether it was. He had a prepared statement, bullet points, and an insight in the second paragraph about closed primary systems that six months of research had produced and that nobody in this room was going to quote.

He also had a faint and stubborn hope. He carried it the way you carry an umbrella in a city where it almost never rains: awkwardly, and unwilling to leave it behind.

PRINCESS MONSTER TRUCK, of Baton Rouge, Louisiana: Twelve-year hospitality veteran. Driver of a 2009 Chevy Silverado with a cracked windshield that insurance had classified as “cosmetic damage” three times, in language so precisely calibrated to the appearance of reasonableness that she had read each letter twice, certain she’d missed where they were wrong.

She had a manila folder containing three claim denials, two formal appeals, one letter from a state regulator that deployed four paragraphs to say nothing, and a printout of Louisiana House Bill 423 annotated in red pen with words that were, legally speaking, opinions and, morally speaking, verdicts.

She placed the folder on the table and rested both hands flat on top of it.

She did not think of herself as someone who had lost control of her circumstances. She thought of herself as someone who had documented them. This distinction was, for the moment, load-bearing.

THE PROCEEDINGS

CHAIRMAN NEW WASHING MACHINE: The Committee will come to order — using “order” in the loosest possible sense, given that the fundamental ordering principle of this institution appears to be the organized redistribution of resources from people who have less to people who have more, while everyone involved describes it as something else and produces documentation to support the description. I recognize the Senator from Delaware.

SENATOR SHMURPLE-BERRY YOGURT: Thank you, Mr. Chairman. It is a genuine pleasure to be here.

[He said this the way a man says something he has said so many times the words have worn smooth, like river stones — not false, exactly, just no longer sharp enough to catch on anything.]

The American people—

PRINCESS MONSTER TRUCK: I drove seventeen hours.

SENATOR SHMURPLE-BERRY YOGURT: —are the backbone of this great nation—

PRINCESS MONSTER TRUCK: My windshield has a crack in it the size of my forearm and the insurer called it cosmetic.

SENATOR SHMURPLE-BERRY YOGURT: —and your voices are essential to the health of our democracy—

PRINCESS MONSTER TRUCK: I have the letters.

[She placed the folder on the table. It landed with the flat authority of something carried a long distance for a specific purpose.]

[The Senator leaned toward his aide.]

SENATOR SHMURPLE-BERRY YOGURT: (to aide) Can we get her a sedative? Or a cabinet position in the Department of Agriculture?6

CHAIRMAN OLD WASHING MACHINE: Let’s swear in our witnesses and proceed. We have limited time before the next recess, a subsequent recess, and a vote on something that will be described as historic and will not be.

TESTIMONY OF MR. ZUCCHINI EL GOURDO

MR. ZUCCHINI EL GOURDO: I represent — in the sense that I am one of, not in the sense that anyone appointed me — approximately 1.1 million unaffiliated voters in New York City. The fastest-growing registration category in the state. Also legally barred from participating in the primary elections that decide virtually every meaningful race, because New York operates a closed primary, which means the party you declined to join on principle is the party that decides your representation without you.

I want to be clear about what that does to a person over time. It doesn’t make you angry, exactly. Anger implies a belief that things could be otherwise. What it does is more corrosive. It makes you feel like a category error.

[He paused. This was not in the prepared statement.]

I tried to start a nonprofit. Urban gardening, community food access. The average time to obtain the relevant permits in New York City is fourteen months. The average small business fails within its first year. I am a well-educated person with institutional connections and a father whose network includes three former deputy mayors, and the system still felt like a locked room where all the locks were on the inside.

SENATOR SHMURPLE-BERRY YOGURT: Wonderful. And you’re from — Queens?

MR. ZUCCHINI EL GOURDO: Brooklyn.

SENATOR SHMURPLE-BERRY YOGURT: (nodding in a way that suggested Brooklyn confirmed something he had long suspected about Brooklyn) Mm. I had a very good piece of salmon there in 2019. Great borough.

MR. ZUCCHINI EL GOURDO: My father’s venture capital firm did an internal analysis last year. The best return on investment in their entire portfolio was regulatory influence. Buying access to permitting agencies. Better than any direct investment in an actual small business. He showed me the numbers. He thought it was funny.

SENATOR SHMURPLE-BERRY YOGURT: Well — markets—

MR. ZUCCHINI EL GOURDO: It was the least funny thing I’ve ever seen presented on a slide.

[A brief silence. The Senator located several things in his notes that required his attention.]

CHAIRMAN NEW WASHING MACHINE: Fourteen months. You know how long it takes to register a business in Estonia?

MR. ZUCCHINI EL GOURDO: I’ve heard—

CHAIRMAN NEW WASHING MACHINE: Eighteen minutes. (produces a printed Wikipedia page) Estonia looked at the administrative state and made a decision. We looked at the administrative state and added a drainage assessment.

TESTIMONY OF PRINCESS MONSTER TRUCK

PRINCESS MONSTER TRUCK: Are we done with the polite part?

[She opens the folder.]

I’ve been in hospitality for twelve years. I know food. I know how to hold a line together when it wants to fall apart.

My sister and I have been planning a food truck for three years. Po-boys and smoked boudin. Something ours. So we saved seventeen thousand dollars- we found a truck- we started pulling permits.

[She turned a page. She didn’t need to consult it. The turning was for the record.]

Louisiana requires a state health certificate, a city business license, a fire inspection certification, and proof of a licensed commissary kitchen. You cannot substitute your own kitchen — even commercial-grade — because the certification costs four thousand dollars and takes four to seven months. Then a Department of Revenue seller’s permit, sales tax registration, and in New Orleans a separate French Quarter vendor permit for any street within two blocks that costs twelve hundred dollars and has a waiting list that hasn’t moved since 2022.

Meanwhile: Governor Landry signs “No Pay, No Play.” The $100,000 auto threshold. The Housley Presumption, gone. You tell us it stops frivolous lawsuits. My premiums went up fifteen percent the following quarter. I am currently making decisions about my alternator and my insulin that I should not be making.

Twenty-three percent turnout in the last Louisiana general election. The people who were supposed to be in our corner have spent so long in rooms like this one that they’ve stopped being able to tell the difference between representing us and performing representing us.

[She closed the folder.]

That’s what I wanted to say about the right to start a business.

THE SYNTHESIS7

[A silence formed of the specific kind that accumulates when something true has been said in a space architecturally designed for the management of truth rather than its direct expression. The fluorescent lights hummed. They had hummed through Vietnam and Watergate and the deficit and the collapse and a number of working groups whose white papers were filed in rooms that no longer existed. They had no opinions. This was, in equal measure, a comfort and not a comfort at all.]

SENATOR SHMURPLE-BERRY YOGURT: The commissary kitchen requirement is — yes, that’s something we’ve been actively—

PRINCESS MONSTER TRUCK: With what outcome?

SENATOR SHMURPLE-BERRY YOGURT: —examining, as part of a broader working group—

PRINCESS MONSTER TRUCK: What working group?

SENATOR SHMURPLE-BERRY YOGURT: —that is in the process of—

PRINCESS MONSTER TRUCK: I drove seventeen hours.

SENATOR SHMURPLE-BERRY YOGURT: (quietly) Yes. You’ve mentioned.

PRINCESS MONSTER TRUCK: I’ll keep mentioning it until something moves.

[The Senator looked at the folder. His second instrument — the tuning fork, the underexercised one — registered something it couldn’t immediately classify. Not guilt. Something prior to guilt. Something that would become guilt if he let it develop.]

SENATOR SHMURPLE-BERRY YOGURT: The working families of this country deserve a regulatory framework that—

[A fraction of a second. The kind of pause that in a different story would be the moment everything changed. He looked, briefly, like a man who had just heard himself — not the words, which were the right words, which were exactly the words the moment required — but the mechanism. The fluency. The phrase arriving fully formed without any intermediate step that could be called deciding.]

[Princess Monster Truck noticed. She didn’t say anything. She just watched.]

[Then the moment passed, back into the general current of things, and he was a five-term Senator again.]

CHAIRMAN NEW WASHING MACHINE: Here is what I keep arriving at.

[He set down his pen.]

You both came here. You know how this system works — or doesn’t — better than most people in this building who are paid to know it. You’re asking the state to move its foot off the hose.8

Instead this issue is going to go to a working group. Then a subcommittee. Then a recess. Then an election cycle. Then a new working group that won’t have read the notes from the old one because those notes are in a format the current document system no longer supports. And somewhere in Louisiana a man who owns a yacht called Tax Shelter will file his quarterly returns in a condition of profound personal comfort, and he will not think about the food truck once.

SENATOR SHMURPLE-BERRY YOGURT: That is an uncharitable characterization of the legislative process.

CHAIRMAN OLD WASHING MACHINE: Is it wrong?

[The lights hummed. Somewhere down the hall, a lobbyist was having an excellent lunch and not thinking about the people who prepared it.]

ADJOURNMENT

CHAIRMAN OLD WASHING MACHINE: The Committee will take these testimonies under advisement. We will produce a white paper. It will be twelve pages. Seven people will read past page four. The rest of the country will continue to stare at the flickering lights of their dwindling bank accounts and draw their own conclusions, several of which will be correct. Meeting adjourned.[^meeting_adjourned]

ADDENDUM: SUBSEQUENT EVENTS

UNDERNEATH MANHATTAN, NY

On the train back to Brooklyn — delayed, indefinitely, for reasons the MTA described as “signal issues” and everyone understood as something much older that nobody had fixed — Zucchini El Gourdo opened his laptop. Not a policy brief. Not the Substack post he’d been unable to finish because he couldn’t find an ending that felt honest rather than performed. A letter. To someone who knew a city councilperson who had been asking, apparently, why the unaffiliated numbers kept climbing and nobody showed up — asking as if she actually wanted to know.

TUSCALOOSA, AL

On the drive back to Louisiana, Princess Monster Truck called her sister.

“We’re still doing it,” she said.

“After all that?”

“Especially after all that. Pull the actual enforcement records on commissary certification — not the statute, the enforcement. I want to know what’s real.”

“You think there’s a gap?”

“There’s always a gap. It’s where we live.”

The crack in the windshield caught the last of the sunset and turned it briefly into something that looked almost like a map.

She drove south, into the dark.

She was, as far as she could tell, the one driving.

The food truck opened in the spring. It was called THE SEVENTEENTH HOUR. The boudin was excellent.

It was not the only one.

Across the country, in the gaps between statutes and enforcement, between the official record and the footnotes, between the theater and the thing the theater was supposed to represent, people were doing the same quiet arithmetic: the cost of asking permission versus the cost of not asking, the distance between the working group and the actual work, the specific weight of a system that processes every genuine thing into the appearance of a genuine thing and files the paperwork correctly and schedules the follow-up meeting and means, on balance, for none of this to change.

And then they were opening food trucks. And mutual aid networks. And neighborhood land trusts. And small schools in living rooms. And local currencies backed by nothing except the agreement of people who had decided to agree. They were building, in the negative space of the official system, the faint outline of a different one — not loudly, not with manifestos, not in ways that would be covered or co-opted or reframed as extremism — just practically, the way people build things when they have stopped waiting for permission and started asking a different question entirely.

The question was not: how do we fix this?

The question was: what do we do instead?

Nobody called it a revolution. Revolutions have press coverage and faces and the kind of drama that gets someone’s name on a stamp. This didn’t have any of that. It had boudin and a waiting list that hadn’t moved since 2022 and eleven thousand dollars saved at eighty dollars a month and a crack in a windshield that caught the light in a way that looked, if you were driving south into the dark and feeling something that wasn’t quite hope but was in the neighborhood, almost like a map.

The working group met for the first time on a Thursday in April. There were eleven members. Three had previously worked for the Louisiana Restaurant Association. One had written the original commissary certification statute in 2004. The agenda was thorough. The minutes would be available upon request.

Nobody requested them.

The lights hummed on.

In the theater, and elsewhere.

Mostly elsewhere.
